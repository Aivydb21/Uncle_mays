# Comparables and Underwriting Memo

**Project:** Uncle May's Produce, North Lawndale Food Hub
**Site:** 3726 W 16th St, Chicago, IL 60623
**Format:** 3,000 sq ft neighborhood grocery + prepared foods, with Months 1 to 3 produce-box delivery preceding retail open
**Capital ask:** approximately $750K total (approximately $250K produce-delivery launch, approximately $500K retail buildout and operation)
**Audience:** Steans Family Foundation diligence team
**Author:** Uncle May's Produce LLC
**Date:** May 2026

---

## Purpose

This memo grounds every line of the Uncle May's North Lawndale financial model in published industry benchmarks, comparable store data, and verified North Lawndale market evidence. It is written to be readable on its own and to support both the financial model and the funding deck. Where a benchmark is contested or paywalled, that is flagged in the data confidence section at the end.

The prior versions of the financial model and pitch deck were not grounded in research. This memo replaces them with sourced data so that a CDFI or foundation research team can audit each assumption against a public source.

---

## Executive Summary

1. **The trade area is real and underserved.** The site sits in a USDA Low Income Low Access census tract with approximately 36,000 residents in the North Lawndale community area, approximately 41 percent of whom live in households without a vehicle. Within 1.5 miles there is one full-service grocer (Freshway Market on Roosevelt) plus an operationally troubled Save A Lot at Pulaski. The corridor is on the North Lawndale Community Coordinating Council Quality of Life Plan as a named priority.

2. **The right revenue benchmark for a 3,000 sq ft underserved-market grocery is $300 to $550 per sq ft, building over 18 to 24 months.** The earlier model's $188 per sq ft figure is below every published benchmark and reads as a Year 1 ramp number presented as a target. A defensible ramp is approximately $600K to $900K Year 1, $1.0M to $1.2M Year 2, $1.2M to $1.5M Year 3.

3. **Year 1 headcount of 7 to 9 FTE is correct for the revenue band.** The earlier 15 to 20 FTE claim implies under $40K of revenue per FTE, which is half the worst published independent grocery operator. Either revenue moves up or headcount moves down. Tying hiring to revenue triggers solves both.

4. **The existing equipment in the Muldrow space is the project's economic lever.** Wild Onion Market, the closest size and format comparable in Chicago, raised approximately $3.25M for a 3,500 sq ft co-op grocery built into a vanilla shell. Forty Acres Fresh Market in Austin neighborhood disclosed approximately $11.8M of subsidy capital for a 9,700 sq ft historic-building rehab. Uncle May's $750K all-in is materially below both, and only credible because the Muldrow space is not a white box. Documenting which equipment is on-site, its condition, and its remaining useful life is the diligence step that converts the $750K number from optimistic to defensible.

5. **Steans is on-thesis.** Steans Family Foundation is a founding co-funder of the North Lawndale Fresh collaborative (with Builders Initiative, Lumpkin Family Foundation, Walter S. Mander Foundation, and Food:Land:Opportunity), whose explicit 2022 to 2026 goal is to expand healthy retail and grow food enterprises in North Lawndale. Uncle May's is squarely within that thesis.

6. **The biggest gap in the prior model is reserves.** CDFI underwriters require a 6 to 12 month operating reserve and a working capital line for cash conversion cycle drag during the Year 1 ramp. Together that is approximately $160K to $300K of capital that was missing from the earlier $400K and $800K versions. That gap, plus the existing equipment offset, is what reconciles the $750K ask to the $945K to $1.13M industry bottom-up build.

### 1.5 Wallet sizing for revenue grounding

USDA Thrifty Food Plan, FY2024 reference family of four: approximately $973 per month, which is also the SNAP maximum allotment basis. BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey 2023, lowest income quintile: approximately $3,500 to $3,800 per consumer unit per year on food at home, or approximately $67 to $73 per week. North Lawndale households at $32K household income sit between BLS quintiles 1 and 2, which produces a defensible local food-at-home spend of approximately $70 to $95 per week per household, with seasonal peaks at $100 to $110.

Total food-at-home wallet in the North Lawndale community area:

- 36,200 residents divided by approximately 2.6 household size equals approximately 13,900 households
- 13,900 households times $80 per week times 52 weeks equals **approximately $58M total food-at-home wallet** in the community area

A 3,000 sq ft store at $1.0M to $1.2M stabilized revenue captures approximately 1.7 to 2.1 percent of community area wallet. A $1.5M Year 3 stabilized revenue is 2.6 percent of community area wallet. These are achievable capture rates given the competitive set; Wild Onion in Rogers Park targets a 4 percent capture of local residents as the published viability threshold for their footprint.

A 15 to 25 percent SNAP and EBT share of total sales is the realistic assumption for this income mix. EBT acceptance must be live on day one of retail.

Sources: [BLS Consumer Expenditures 2023](https://www.bls.gov/opub/reports/consumer-expenditures/2023/), [USDA Food Plans Monthly Cost reports](https://www.fns.usda.gov/research/cnpp/usda-food-plans/cost-food-monthly-reports), [Cost of Food, Thrifty Food Plan, January 2025](https://fns-prod.azureedge.us/sites/default/files/resource-files/Cost_Of_Food_Thrifty_Food_Plan_January_2025.pdf).

### 2.5 Cash conversion cycle

Chain grocery operates a famously negative cash conversion cycle (vendors fund working capital). Independents in Year 1 do not. Most produce and DSD vendors require COD or Net 7 for new accounts.

| Component | Independent Year 1 | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Days Inventory Outstanding, blended | 20 to 30 days (3 to 7 fresh, 30 to 45 shelf-stable) | csimarket.com, HBS Online |
| Days Payable Outstanding, blended | 15 to 35 days; produce often Net 7 to 14, dry goods Net 15 to 30 | Bill.com, industry standard |
| Days Sales Outstanding | 0 to 1 day (cash and card retail) | Industry standard |
| Net cash conversion cycle | **+5 to +15 days** for independents in Year 1, narrowing to negative as vendor terms improve over 12 to 18 months | Inferred from independent grocer practice |

**Implication for the Uncle May's capital stack:** approximately $60K to $100K of working capital is required above the opening inventory line for cash conversion drag during Year 1. This was missing from the earlier $400K and $800K capital plans.

Sources: [Ramp on cash conversion cycle](https://ramp.com/blog/how-to-calculate-cash-conversion-cycle), [HBS Online primer](https://online.hbs.edu/blog/post/word-of-the-week-cash-conversion-cycle), [csimarket grocery efficiency](https://csimarket.com/Industry/industry_Efficiency.php?ind=1305).

### 2.6 Inventory turnover

| Format | Turns per year |
|---|---|
| Independent grocery | 8 to 12 |
| Large supermarket chains | 15 to 20-plus |
| Fresh and perishable categories | 15 to 50 |
| Shelf-stable and center store | 5 to 10 |
| Industry blended median | approximately 14 |

At 12 turns per year, $100K opening inventory supports approximately $1.2M annual COGS, which at 36 percent blended GM supports approximately $1.9M in revenue. **The $100K opening inventory line is more than enough for Year 1 ramp revenue and is correctly framed as opening assortment investment, not steady-state inventory.** Steady-state inventory at $1.0M revenue and 12 turns is closer to $50K. The $50K differential liquidates to cash within the first 6 to 9 months and is a working-capital tailwind that should be modeled explicitly.

### 2.7 Breakeven sales and ramp to breakeven

A 3,000 sq ft urban grocery with prepared foods needs approximately $900K to $1.2M of annualized run-rate revenue to break even, based on a $300 to $400 per sq ft breakeven benchmark from CDFI underwriting practice.

Typical ramp to breakeven for new independents in underserved markets: **18 to 36 months.** ProPublica's 2024 reporting found that 5 of 24 federally-funded food-desert stores from the 2020 to 2021 cohort had failed by 2024. The Reinvestment Fund's published view is that CDFI-financed grocery in food-access areas has materially better survival when the deal includes a working-capital reserve and a predevelopment grant. Operators without reserves are the operators that fail.

**Uncle May's model should show explicit operating loss in Year 1, breakeven by month 18 to 24, and a 6 to 12 month operating reserve funded at close.** That structure mirrors what Reinvestment Fund and HFFI underwrite to.

Sources: [ProPublica on food desert grocery](https://www.propublica.org/article/food-desert-grocery-store-cairo-illinois), [CDFI Fund Underwriting Supermarkets](https://www.cdfifund.gov/sites/cdfi/files/documents/underwriting-supermarkets_for-fund_102411.pdf), [Reinvestment Fund Underwriting Supermarkets insights page](https://www.reinvestment.com/insights/underwriting-supermarkets/).

Wild Onion's $930 per sq ft all-in capital intensity is the **vanilla-shell, no-equipment-offset benchmark**. Uncle May's $750K total project cost equals $250 per sq ft, which is materially below Wild Onion. The reconciliation is, again, the existing built-out space and equipment in the Muldrow location.

### 3.2 IMAN Go Green Community Fresh Market, Englewood, Chicago. Positive operator comp.

- **Footprint:** estimated 3,000 to 5,000 sq ft (not publicly disclosed)
- **Location:** 1207 W 63rd St, Englewood, the same corridor where Whole Foods closed in 2022
- **Operator:** Inner-City Muslim Action Network (IMAN), 501(c)(3)
- **Opened:** March 2022
- **Capital stack:** multi-funder coalition including Healthy Communities Foundation; specific stack not publicly itemized
- **Performance signals at the 3-year mark, 2025:**
  - 6,700-plus rewards members
  - 25-plus vendors of color partnered as in-store sellers
  - 52,000 redemptions of "Distinguished Resident Discount"
  - Serves over 6,000 residents with fresh produce
- **Status:** Open and expanding

**Lessons applicable to Uncle May's:**

- A non-traditional layout (no bulletproof glass, no Flamin' Hots prominence) is a measurable trust signal in a previously distrustful corridor
- Vendor-of-color shelf partnerships create both supply diversity and community equity buy-in (this maps directly to Uncle May's Black Vendors Airtable base)
- A 3-year rewards member count of 6,700 is roughly 18 to 25 percent of the immediate residential trade area, which is a credible target for Uncle May's by Year 3

Sources: [IMAN Go Green](https://imancentral.org/fresh-market/), [WBEZ on Go Green opening](https://www.wbez.org/race-class-communities/2022/03/08/iman-opens-go-green-community-fresh-market-in-englewood), [Next City](https://nextcity.org/urbanist-news/in-chicagos-food-deserts-community-led-markets-fill-the-void-as-big-chains).

### 3.3 Brown's Super Stores, Philadelphia. Positive operator philosophy comp.

- **Format:** Full-service supermarkets, ShopRite and Fresh Grocer banners. Generally 40,000 to 60,000 sq ft each. **Wrong scale, right philosophy.**
- **Stores:** 13 at peak. Founded 1988. Owner: Jeffrey Brown
- **Differentiator:** Pop-up kiosks and supplier slots reserved for Black-owned brands inside the stores, plus hiring of formerly incarcerated workers
- **Track record:** 30-plus years of full-service supermarkets operating profitably in low-income predominantly Black Philadelphia neighborhoods, when most other operators have exited
- **Lesson for Uncle May's:** Kiosks and in-store partnerships with Black-owned brands are a proven traffic and authenticity driver. This applies directly to Uncle May's prepared foods and Black vendor strategy.

Sources: [Inquirer on Brown's ShopRite Black-owned partnerships](https://www.inquirer.com/food/shoprite-supermarket-partnership-black-owned-food-business-20210410.html), [Philadelphia Citizen profile](https://thephiladelphiacitizen.org/jeff-brown/).

### 3.4 Daily Table, Boston. Closed 2025. Failure-mode comp.

- **Format:** Nonprofit small-format grocery, 3,000 to 6,000 sq ft per store
- **Founder:** Doug Rauch, former president of Trader Joe's
- **History:** Founded 2012, first store opened 2015, 4 stores at peak
- **Funding model:** Nonprofit. 70 percent earned revenue, 30 percent philanthropy at peak
- **Customers served:** 260,000-plus residents in 2024, the final full year, up 24 percent year-over-year
- **Closed:** All stores closed May 2025 after 10 years of operation

**Stated reasons for closure:**

1. Food prices rose 23.6 percent from 2020 to 2024, which eroded the discount margin
2. Sales fell 40 percent during the pandemic
3. Loss of federal funding
4. Tightened philanthropy environment

**Lessons:** A 70 percent earned-revenue, 30 percent philanthropy model is not sustainable at steady state in a high-food-inflation environment. The deeper the discount commitment, the more fragile the model. Daily Table's own post-mortem prescribed: lock in farm partnerships (Pembroke), add catering and prepared foods revenue (a planned Uncle May's category), build endowment, secure city contracts.

**Why Uncle May's is structurally different:** for-profit equity discipline; prepared foods margin layer at 45 to 60 percent gross; Pembroke direct sourcing; subsidized occupancy that holds fixed costs near zero in Months 1 to 3.

Sources: [Boston.com on Daily Table closing](https://www.boston.com/news/local-news/2025/05/12/daily-table-grocery-stores-closing/), [Grocery Dive](https://www.grocerydive.com/news/daily-table-boston-grocery-closing/747719/), [Daily Table Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daily_Table).

### 3.5 Fare and Square, Chester PA. Failure-mode comp, the canonical nonprofit grocery cautionary tale.

- **Footprint:** approximately 16,000 sq ft (rehab of an abandoned grocery)
- **Operator:** Philabundance regional food bank, declared first U.S. nonprofit supermarket
- **Opened:** September 2013
- **Capital stack disclosed:** $7M New Markets Tax Credit financing via Reinvestment Fund and Nonprofit Finance Fund; $1.3M bridge loan via Reinvestment Fund (Pennsylvania Fresh Food Financing Initiative plus federal HFFI). **Total approximately $8.3M-plus.**
- **Economics:** Sales grew year-over-year but the store needed approximately 20 percent more revenue to break even. Subsidized by Philabundance throughout life. Sold to Cousin's Fresh Market in 2018; Cousin's wound down operations afterward.

**Lesson:** Even with $8M of subsidy, a nonprofit grocery in a low-density low-income market needed 20 percent more sales to break even. The "nonprofit supermarket" model carries the cost structure of a supermarket without the volume of a supermarket. **Reinvestment Fund's own profile of the project named location, transit, and density as the binding constraints.** This is the comp Steans-class funders know by heart.

**Why Uncle May's is structurally different:** smaller footprint and lower fixed cost; for-profit equity discipline; prepared foods margin layer; denser North Lawndale daytime population than Chester; transit-anchored walk-up trade area.

Sources: [NPR on Fare and Square](https://www.npr.org/sections/thesalt/2016/07/06/484819718/a-nonprofit-grocer-tries-to-sell-healthier-food-without-going-under), [WHYY](https://whyy.org/articles/chesters-non-profit-food-market-tries-to-square-mission-with-bottom-line/), [PolicyLink profile](https://www.policylink.org/resources/library/profile-fare-and-square), [NMTC Coalition](https://nmtccoalition.org/project/fare-and-square/).

### 3.6 Englewood Whole Foods, Chicago. Closed 2022. Failure-mode comp.

- **Footprint:** 18,000 sq ft
- **Location:** 832 W 63rd St, Englewood (predominantly Black South Side)
- **Opened:** September 2016. Closed November 2022, six years
- **Capital:** approximately $10M-plus in City of Chicago subsidies including TIF (consensus reporting band)

**Stated reasons for closure:**

1. Prices too high for the neighborhood income profile
2. Low traffic, "often empty even on Saturdays" per then-Mayor Lightfoot
3. Post-Amazon-acquisition (2017) corporate retrenchment, deprioritized the experiment
4. No advance warning to the city to course-correct

**Lessons:** Wrong assortment and wrong price tier kills traffic regardless of footprint and subsidy. Out-of-market operator (Austin TX based Whole Foods, then Amazon) lacked a feedback loop with the neighborhood. The closure was widely cited as evidence that Black neighborhoods cannot support grocery, which is a framing Uncle May's needs to rebut directly.

**Why Uncle May's is structurally different:** right-sized footprint at 3,000 vs 18,000 sq ft; neighborhood-priced assortment indexed to local basket size; owner-operator on the ground with Matt Weschler; vendor partnerships with Black-owned brands; Pembroke direct sourcing.

Sources: [Block Club on Whole Foods Englewood closing](https://blockclubchicago.org/2022/11/14/whole-foods-closes-englewood-store-6-years-after-promising-to-bring-fresh-food-to-the-south-side/), [Grocery Dive](https://www.grocerydive.com/news/what-grocers-can-learn-from-whole-foods-controversial-exit-from-a-chicago/638476/).

### 3.7 Yellow Banana / Save A Lot, Chicago. Failure-mode comp.

In April 2023 the City of Chicago committed $13.5M plus $13M private capital, $26.5M total, to Yellow Banana for six South and West Side Save A Lot stores. Fourteen months later, zero stores were open. By August 2024 the operator could not pay an $800 utility bill. By late 2024, three stores had reopened, including the one at 420 S Pulaski Rd within 1.0 mi of the Uncle May's site.

This is the canonical "subsidy without operator execution" failure mode. Steans diligence will know it.

**Why Uncle May's is structurally different:** Matt Weschler is the operator on day one, not after the capital is deployed. Pre-commitment milestones with public reporting. Board oversight including the lead investor. Phase 1 produce-box delivery generates revenue from Month 1, before any retail buildout, which is operational evidence the operator can execute.

Sources: [WBEZ on Yellow Banana](https://www.wbez.org/business/2025/02/10/savealot-yellow-banana-city-funding-complaints-overflowing-dumpsters-rats), [Sun-Times editorial](https://chicago.suntimes.com/editorials/2024/05/30/save-a-lot-yellow-banana-food-desert-groceries-chicago-south-west-sides-editorial), [Block Club on Save A Lot funding](https://blockclubchicago.org/2023/11/01/yellow-banana-save-a-lot-grocery-stores-got-26-5-million-neighbors-are-still-waiting-for-upgraded-stores/).

### 3.8 Super Pride, Baltimore. Closed 2000. Historical Black-owned comp.

- **Founder:** Charles Thurgood Burns, took over bankrupt Super Jet Market 1970
- **Peak:** 7 to 8 stores, $43M annual sales, 400-plus employees, Top 100 Black-owned business in the U.S., #3 independent grocer in Baltimore
- **Closed:** All 8 stores closed by November 2000

**Stated reasons:** competition from national chains, population decline in Baltimore (approximately 25 percent over the relevant period), "same stores fighting for fewer dollars" per the then-CEO.

**Lesson:** The Black-owned grocery chain failure mode is sub-scale defense against national chains in a shrinking population corridor. Uncle May's avoids this by starting neighborhood-scale, not chain-scale, and by targeting a North Lawndale corridor that public-policy and philanthropic funders are actively reinvesting in (the North Lawndale Fresh fund of which Steans is a member).

Naming Super Pride in the deck shows the team has done historical homework. Steans's foundation peers cite it often.

## Section 4. CDFI and foundation underwriting framework

This is the analytical structure Steans's research director will expect to see in the financial model. The framework below is drawn from the CDFI Fund's *Underwriting Supermarkets and Grocery Stores* (Reinvestment Fund authored), the HFFI Pennsylvania Fresh Food Financing Initiative reporting standard, and LISC Chicago's INVEST South/West underwriting practice.

### 4.1 Required pro forma format

- 5-year monthly pro forma for Year 1, then annual for Years 2 through 5
- Line-item P and L expressed both in dollars and as percent of sales
- Sources and Uses with explicit construction draw schedule tied to architect milestones
- Working capital model separate from the P and L: opening inventory, days payable, days receivable, credit card float
- Sensitivity tables on (a) sales ramp speed, (b) gross margin, and (c) labor as percent of sales
- **Three-scenario presentation: base, downside, stress**, not just base case
- Debt schedule with separate amortization for each tranche

### 4.2 Coverage ratios and operating benchmarks

| Metric | Expected level |
|---|---|
| Debt Service Coverage Ratio, stabilized Year 2-plus | at least 1.20x, preferred 1.25 to 1.35x |
| DSCR Year 1 | at least 1.0x acceptable if operating reserve covers shortfall |
| Operating reserve at opening | 6 to 12 months of opex held in cash |
| Gross margin, blended | 22 to 30 percent default; 35-plus only with documented prepared foods and private label mix |
| Sales per sq ft, small-format urban underserved | $400 to $700 typical; underserved markets cluster lower in band in Year 1 |
| EBITDA margin, stabilized small-format | 3 to 6 percent industry; 8 to 12 percent best-in-class with prepared foods |
| Inventory turns | 14 to 18 for full-line, 20-plus produce-heavy |
| Labor as percent of sales | 12 to 15 percent target; greater than 17 percent is a red flag |
| Occupancy as percent of sales | 4 to 5 percent or less (this is often the line that kills underserved deals) |
| Shrink | 2 to 4 percent acceptable; greater than 5 percent is a red flag |
| Leverage ratio (private dollars to public dollars) | 5:1 or better is HFFI threshold |

### 4.3 Required impact metrics

These will appear in the LOI and likely as covenants in any deployment:

- Population served within half-mile and 1-mile radius, with percent low-income (LMI) and percent USDA Low Access designation
- Jobs created (FTE) and jobs retained, with breakdown by full-time and part-time and percent of staff living within the store zip code
- Hourly wage floor and average wage versus local living wage benchmark
- Sourcing percent from minority-owned vendors, local farmers, and Black producers
- Households served per month (transactions times average basket, translated to unique households)
- SNAP and EBT acceptance and SNAP dollars as percent of sales
- Health and diet outcome proxies (PA FFFI tracked obesity rate change in catchment; Uncle May's would commit to a baseline survey and three-year follow-up)

Sources: [CDFI Fund Underwriting Supermarkets PDF](https://www.cdfifund.gov/sites/cdfi/files/documents/underwriting-supermarkets_for-fund_102411.pdf), [Reinvestment Fund Underwriting Supermarkets](https://www.reinvestment.com/insights/underwriting-supermarkets/), [The Food Trust PA Fresh Food Financing Initiative](https://thefoodtrust.org/what-we-do/hffi/pa/), [HFFI 2024 Planning Grant RFA](https://www.investinginfood.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/HFFI-2024-Planning-Grant-Request-for-Applications.pdf).
