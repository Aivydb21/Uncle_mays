# Why This Works Where Others Failed

## Naming the comparable failures and explaining the structural differences

**Englewood Whole Foods (closed 2022).** Wrong assortment, wrong price tier, absentee corporate operator. *Different here:* Owner-operator on the ground. Neighborhood-priced assortment indexed to local basket size. Black vendor partnerships. 3,000 sq ft, not 18,000.

**Daily Table Boston (all 4 stores closed May 2025).** Discount-anchored nonprofit, exposed to food inflation, philanthropy-dependent. *Different here:* For-profit equity discipline. Prepared foods margin layer at 45-60 percent gross. Pembroke direct sourcing. Subsidized occupancy holding fixed costs near zero in Months 1-3.

**Fare & Square Chester PA.** $8.3M of subsidy capital. Needed 20 percent more sales to break even. *Different here:* Right-sized footprint. Lower fixed cost. Denser North Lawndale daytime population than Chester. Transit-anchored walk-up trade area.

**Save A Lot / Yellow Banana (Chicago).** $26.5M committed in April 2023; 14 months later, zero stores open; couldn't pay an $800 utility bill. *Different here:* Operator on Day 1. Phase 1 produce-box business generates revenue from Month 1, before any retail buildout. The operator is on the ground earning trust and revenue from Day 1, not waiting on capital to act.

# Stress Tested

## What happens if revenue misses by 15 to 30 percent

**Downside case** (revenue -15%, gross margin -3 points)

- Year 1 EBITDA: -$53K
- Year 2 EBITDA: -$55K
- Cumulative Y1-Y2 burn: ~$108K
- Year 3 EBITDA: $125K (recovery)
- **Operating reserve covers the burn. Steans capital is not at risk in this case.**

**Stress case** (revenue -30%, gross margin -5 points, labor +2 points of revenue)

- Cumulative Y1-Y3 burn: ~$525K
- This is the line where additional capital would be needed
- Honest answer: we would either secure follow-on funding or wind down operations rather than burn further

This is documented in the financial model. Steans diligence can flex any input.

| Use | Instrument | Tranche | Amount |

**Why this structure works for Steans**

- **Workforce grants** map cleanly to Steans's stated NLW priority of workforce development, job creation, economic opportunity. Grocery is the vehicle, not the program.
- **Subordinated debt** below market (4-6 percent) preserves operator equity and avoids the SAFE structure that's wrong for this audience.
- **Line of credit** funds working capital drag without sitting dead on the balance sheet.

# Tranche 1 Use of Funds, Detailed

## What $250K activates in the first 90 days

**Equipment supplements ($50K)**
Refrigeration top-up to in-place units, packing tables, delivery-vehicle outfitting, scales, label printers, basic POS for box subscriptions.

**Initial inventory ($70K)**
90-day produce supply from Pembroke and complementary suppliers, dry goods for box assortment, packaging materials.

**Staffing net of grant ($35K)**
5 to 7 local hires at $16+/hr through Month 3, then 6 to 7 through Month 6. Workforce development grant absorbs $100K of Phase 1 gross labor.

**Working capital reserve ($30K)**
Funds cash conversion cycle drag during ramp. Independents pay vendors COD or Net 7 in Year 1; vendor terms earn out over 12-18 months.

**Outreach, marketing, permits, legal ($30K)**
Community launch event, social media, NLW press relationships, business registrations, retail food permits, EBT/SNAP retailer authorization, legal.

**Software, POS, infrastructure ($20K)**
Subscription management system, route optimization for delivery, basic POS for both subscription and walk-up sales, accounting setup.

**Contingency ($15K)**
6 percent of Tranche 1.
