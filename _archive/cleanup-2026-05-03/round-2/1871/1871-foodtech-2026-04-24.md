## 🎯 The frame — lead with this

**Uncle May's is a retail-first platform.** The website is the customer-discovery + pre-opening subscription channel. It is NOT the primary revenue thesis.

If a VC asks about website metrics before you've set that frame, redirect:

> "The website is a supporting channel. Our primary thesis is the flagship retail store, which the site data is helping us validate."

**Do not pitch this as D2C produce delivery.** You will be compared to Misfits Market ($500M loss), Imperfect Foods ($200M loss), Blue Apron (bankrupt). You are Cardenas ($1B exit), Patel Brothers (60 stores), Eataly ($2–3B rev) — the **first** ethnic grocery chain for Black America.

## 14 anticipated questions with prepared answers

### Website / traffic / conversion

**Q: What's your site traffic?**
A: About 3,800 sessions a month, growing 10x month-over-month on modest ad spend. Most traffic is mobile-first from paid social — standard for pre-launch where we're validating messaging, not optimizing conversion.

**Q: What's your conversion rate?**
A: Honestly, the site is underperforming right now — around 0.2% — and we know exactly why. Our Meta ads were deep-linking past the homepage into checkout, which skips the product story. We just fixed that this week, along with a measurement issue that was under-reporting conversions by about 80%. The real read is the next 30 days. But I want to stress: the website is a marketing channel, not our primary revenue thesis. The business is the retail store.

**Q: How many paying customers do you have?**
A: On the e-commerce side, 17 Stripe customers from about $500 in cumulative ad spend. That's not our traction story — that's customer discovery pre-launch. The traction story is the site: $8 million a year, three-year track record. We're taking over a real business, not starting from scratch.

**Q: What's your CAC?**
A: Early number is $30–$50 at current ad spend. The relevant number is retail CAC, which is effectively near-zero — foot traffic in Hyde Park is already there. E-commerce CAC is a pre-launch marketing cost, not an ongoing unit-economics driver.

**Q: What's your LTV?**
A: Blended e-commerce LTV is about $270 at 35% GM — $94 of GP per customer. Retail LTV is different — targeting $1,200+ annual spend per household at the flagship.

**Q: Your Meta ad account is having problems — what's going on?**
A: Meta put our ad account under review this week — standard for fast-scaling new brands in food categories. We're resolving it and have a Google Search Ads backup ready. Paid social is not mission-critical. Our primary GTM is the physical store.

### Traction / trajectory

**Q: Why so few customers?**
A: We're pre-flagship. Our energy has been on the store — site LOI, SBA approval, team, architect, designer. The e-commerce runs to validate messaging and seed a subscriber list for opening day, not to hit a revenue target. When the doors open we'll have a warm list.

**Q: What's your growth trajectory?**
A: Site traffic grew 10x in the last 30 days — from 350 to 3,800 sessions. Revenue is lumpy because we deliver one day a week. The real trajectory inflection is flagship opening — targeting inside 12 months of funding close.

### Business model / moat

**Q: Why can't Whole Foods or Kroger just do this?**
A: Cultural authenticity, multi-generational community trust, and exclusive Black-supplier relationships are not things Kroger can simulate. A Midwestern chain tried to target the Indian demographic in the 90s — Patel Brothers is still standing.

**Q: How is this different from Cardenas or El Super?**
A: Same playbook, different demographic. Those chains proved the model for Hispanic consumers — 70 and 65 stores. Cardenas sold to KKR for a billion. Black Americans represent $123 billion in grocery spend — larger than the Hispanic grocery TAM those chains captured — and there's no dedicated chain. We're running the Cardenas playbook 25 years later for the demographic that was skipped.

**Q: What's your moat once you scale?**
A: Three layers. One: exclusive Black-farmer and Black-CPG supplier network — portable across markets. Two: consumer data — only operator with direct Black consumption signal, licensable to CPGs. Three: brand — culturally authentic grocery brands compound. Eataly is the clearest comp — once they owned the category, no one caught them.

### Capital / use of funds

**Q: Why a SAFE, not priced?**
A: Speed and cost. SBA is the anchor — $2M senior debt, institutional rates, conditionally approved. The SAFE is the last piece of the stack. A priced round at this stage delays close and burns legal spend that belongs in inventory and TI. The $5M cap is below any reasonable Series A comp — we'll price at the next round with store performance data.

**Q: What's the use of proceeds?**
A: $400K equity + $2M SBA = $2.5M capital stack. $1.46M store build-out, $445K soft costs (architecture, legal, permits, PM), $250K initial inventory, $417K SG&A reserve, $60K pre-opening, minus $125K in negotiated AP offset. Every line is bottom-up budgeted with Civic Projects and Food Market Designs.

### Team / risks

**Q: Why you?**
A: Booth MBA, 10 years PE and M&A, deep roots in Black agricultural heritage. But the operator story isn't me — it's the team. COO ran $3B at Amazon. CFO did 20 years at Bank of America. GM started and exited two Chicago grocery stores. I'm the CEO who built the deal. They execute the store.

**Q: Biggest risk?**
A: Execution on the first store — not thesis, not demand, not team. That's why we have a senior operating team. And why we're opening inside an existing $8M-a-year site that already has neighborhood foot traffic. We're doing the riskiest job — store one — on the least risky site.

## When you don't know an answer

Exact line:
> "I don't have that number committed to memory — let me follow up with you by email tomorrow. Can I get your card?"

Then write the question on the back of their card immediately.

**Never fake a number. VCs check.**
