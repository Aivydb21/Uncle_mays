## The problem in Black grocery retail

Three compounding failures in the current system:

**Underserved Black consumers**
Only 35% of Black consumers feel their primary grocer understands their needs (vs. 62% for white consumers, Deloitte). 67% of Black shoppers visit grocery stores monthly — more than any other demographic — yet 15–18% of that spend flows to dollar stores, **driven by access gaps, not preference** (NielsenIQ).

**Invisible Black brands**
40% of Black consumers say their primary store doesn't carry culturally relevant foods. Black-owned CPG brands face structural exclusion from conventional grocery shelves.

**Excluded Black farmers**
Black farmers — 14% of U.S. farmers in 1920, under 2% today — have almost no direct retail pathway into the communities that would most value their product. A $100B demand gap with no dedicated distribution system.

> *"This is a $100B demand problem, with no system of note."*

## The innovation we're piloting

**Uncle May's: a curated, 4,000-SKU grocery format built FOR Black communities, delivered through four linked mechanisms:**

**1. Direct-from-Black-farmer sourcing**
Bypass conventional distributors; procure culturally-relevant produce at the farm level. Keep margin in the Black food economy.

**2. Private-label-first procurement**
Launch with house-branded goods sourced through direct supplier aggregation. Captures margin that conventional grocers surrender to national CPG brands — and passes it to the community as lower prices.

**3. Culturally-aligned curation**
~4,000 SKUs chosen for Black households (vs. 30K–50K in a commodity supermarket), reducing the 2–6 store weekly grocery circuit Black shoppers currently run. The same cost-reduction mechanism that drove Trader Joe's to 3–4x industry-average revenue per square foot — applied to a demographic that is structurally more underserved than Trader Joe's ever was.

**4. Replicable community-rooted format**
7,500–12,000 sqft neighborhood store, walkable and transit-connected, anchored in existing Black community infrastructure. Designed from day one to replicate across underserved Black neighborhoods nationally.

## Equity and inclusion — built into the model, not bolted on

**This is not a Black-branded storefront with conventional procurement.** Equity is the core operating model:

- **Sourcing:** Private-label + farmer-direct procurement means dollars spent at Uncle May's circulate back to Black farmers and Black brand owners instead of leaving the community through conventional distributors
- **Ownership:** Black-founded, Black-led leadership team
- **Shelf access:** Black CPG brands — historically excluded from conventional grocery shelves — are the **default** category assortment, not a specialty section
- **Capital participation:** Community members invited into the ownership and capital structure as the model scales
- **Workforce:** Flagship hiring draws directly from the surrounding Black neighborhood

**This is why the model is not replicable by a conventional grocer.** Cultural authenticity, multi-generational community trust, and exclusive Black-supplier relationships are not things Kroger can simulate.

## Scalability — the replication map

**Three-phase path designed for replication, not for single-store optimization:**

**Phase 1 — Proof of concept (Months 1–18)**
One flagship store, Hyde Park Chicago. Validate the curated-Black-grocery format in a real operating environment. Generate the playbook for replication.

**Phase 2 — Regional dominance (Years 2–4)**
3–10 stores across the top 5 Black American metro markets. 2–3 stores per market to achieve density and local operational efficiency.

**Phase 3 — National expansion (Years 5–10)**
10–100 stores across the top 20 U.S. markets. Category-defining position.

**Why this replicates in underserved communities specifically:**
- Site model is anchored in neighborhood independent-grocery real estate — the same sites currently failing for conventional operators in Black neighborhoods
- Curated 4,000-SKU format requires smaller footprints (7,500–12,000 sqft), making marginal locations viable that a 40K-SKU supermarket couldn't operate in
- Supplier network is portable: once ~20 Black farms and ~50 Black brands are onboarded, the same network serves Chicago, Atlanta, Memphis, Detroit, St. Louis, etc.
- Playbook-based replication, not case-by-case reinvention
