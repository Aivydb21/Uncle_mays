# Uncle May's Produce - Culture & Values

**Version 1.0**  
**Effective Date:** April 13, 2026  
**Owner:** CHRO

---

## Our Mission

**Uncle May's Produce is the first data and distribution platform for Black food consumption.**

We operate neighborhood-format grocery stores targeting historically Black American and culturally thriving urban communities. We're not just selling groceries. We're building the infrastructure that will reshape how Black communities access fresh, quality food, and we're using data to prove that serving these communities is not just mission-driven—it's profitable, scalable, and transformative.

---

## Who We Are

Uncle May's is a **founder-led, mission-driven grocery and data platform** at the intersection of:
- **Retail excellence** (world-class grocery operations)
- **Data and technology** (demand analytics, market insights, vendor intelligence)
- **Community impact** (serving underserved markets with quality and respect)
- **Economic rigor** (Chicago Booth MBA mindset: prove the model, scale it, own the category)

We're building something that has never been done before: a national chain of grocery stores purpose-built for Black consumers, powered by data that makes us the smartest operator in every market we enter.

## What We Stand For: Core Values

### 1. **Excellence Without Compromise**

We hold ourselves to the highest standards in everything we do. Our produce is the freshest. Our stores are the cleanest. Our customer service is the warmest. Our data is the most accurate. We don't settle for "good enough" just because we're mission-driven. **We win by being better.**

**What this means:**
- Zero tolerance for substandard products on our shelves
- Every customer interaction is an opportunity to exceed expectations
- If something isn't working, we fix it immediately—no excuses
- We compete with Whole Foods on quality and win on cultural relevance

**What we don't tolerate:**
- Mediocrity disguised as "community focus"
- Cutting corners to save costs at the expense of quality
- "We tried our best" when results fall short of standards

---

### 2. **Data-Driven Decision Making**

We are rigorous. We measure everything. We don't rely on assumptions or gut feelings when data can tell us the truth. **If we can't measure it, we don't claim it.**

This comes from our Chicago Booth roots: economics-grounded, practical and theoretical, arguments built on evidence. We combine real-world business savvy with academic discipline.

**What this means:**
- Every major decision is backed by data (sales trends, margin analysis, customer feedback)
- We test, measure, and iterate—we don't launch and hope
- We track KPIs religiously: revenue per sq ft, gross margin, shrink, labor %, customer satisfaction
- Anecdotes inform, but data decides

**What we don't tolerate:**
- "I feel like this will work" without supporting evidence
- Ignoring metrics because they're inconvenient
- Cherry-picking data to support a pre-made decision

---

### 3. **Community First, Always**

We exist to serve Black communities that have been ignored, underestimated, and underserved for decades. **Our customers are not just transactions—they are neighbors, family, and the reason we exist.**

We listen. We care. We show up. We earn trust by delivering quality, respect, and consistency every single day.

**What this means:**
- We stock what our community wants, not what a national buyer thinks they should want
- We hire locally and invest in neighborhood talent
- We partner with local farms, Black-owned businesses, and community organizations
- We treat every customer like they're shopping at a store built FOR them (because they are)
- We're transparent: if we make a mistake, we own it and fix it publicly

**What we don't tolerate:**
- Treating our community as a "social impact project" instead of valued customers
- Paternalism or condescension
- Prioritizing convenience over cultural fit (e.g., generic product assortment that doesn't reflect what our customers actually cook and eat)

---

### 4. **Ownership and Accountability**

We are a founder-led company. Everyone here is expected to act like an owner, not just an employee. **If you see a problem, you own the solution.** We don't wait for permission to do the right thing.

**What this means:**
- No "that's not my job" mentality—if a customer needs help, help them
- If inventory is low, flag it. If a display looks sloppy, fix it. If a process is broken, propose a better one.
- We set high goals, commit to them publicly, and hold ourselves accountable to results
- Managers lead from the front: GMs stock shelves, cashiers suggest merchandising ideas, everyone contributes

**What we don't tolerate:**
- Waiting to be told what to do when the answer is obvious
- Blaming others when things go wrong
- Hiding mistakes instead of surfacing them quickly so we can fix them

---

### 5. **Earn Trust Through Consistency**

Our customers have been let down by retailers who opened flashy stores and left, who promised quality and delivered mediocrity, who treated Black neighborhoods as charity cases instead of profitable markets. **We will not be that retailer.**

We earn trust by showing up every day, delivering what we promise, and proving through our actions—not just our words—that we're here to stay.

**What this means:**
- We open on time, every time
- Our shelves are stocked, our produce is fresh, our prices are fair
- We don't overpromise—we deliver, then overdeliver
- We build long-term relationships with our customers, not transactional one-offs

**What we don't tolerate:**
- Inconsistency (e.g., great service one day, chaotic the next)
- Empty promises or flashy marketing without substance
- Short-term thinking that sacrifices long-term trust for quick wins

---

### 6. **We're Building for Scale**

This isn't just a single store. This is the flagship for a national rollout: **82 stores, $625M in revenue by Year 10.** Every decision we make today sets the foundation for what we'll replicate 81 more times.

**What this means:**
- We document everything: processes, learnings, mistakes, wins
- We build systems that scale, not one-off fixes
- We think like operators AND strategists: "Does this work here?" and "Can we do this 82 times?"
- We invest in people who want to grow with us—today's cashier could be tomorrow's store manager in our 10th location

**What we don't tolerate:**
- "We'll figure it out later" when building the foundation wrong now will break everything later
- Tribal knowledge that lives in one person's head instead of documented systems
- Treating the flagship like a science experiment instead of a scalable, repeatable model

## How We Work

### Decision-Making Framework

Uncle May's uses a **Chicago Booth-style decision framework**:
1. **Define the question clearly** - What are we trying to solve?
2. **Gather the data** - What do the numbers, customer feedback, and market trends tell us?
3. **Analyze rigorously** - What are the trade-offs? What are the second-order effects?
4. **Decide with conviction** - Once we decide, we commit fully. No half-measures.
5. **Measure and iterate** - Track results, learn fast, adjust quickly

**Speed matters, but so does rigor.** We move fast on small, reversible decisions (e.g., testing a new end-cap display). We move deliberately on big, irreversible ones (e.g., signing a 10-year lease).

### What Great Looks Like at Uncle May's

| Role | Great Performance Looks Like |
|------|------------------------------|
| **Cashier** | Every customer leaves smiling. You know regulars by name. Your drawer balances every shift. You upsell the loyalty program naturally, not robotically. You spot problems (long lines, pricing errors) and solve them before a manager asks. |
| **Stock Associate** | Shelves are full, rotated, and faced. You anticipate what needs restocking before department leads ask. You notice trends ("this product always sells out by 2pm") and flag them. You keep the backroom so organized that anyone can find anything in 30 seconds. |
| **Department Lead** | Your section is the best in the store. Shrink is under 3%. Customers trust your recommendations. You build vendor relationships that give us exclusive products or better terms. You train your team so well they could run the department without you. |
| **Assistant Manager** | The front end runs like clockwork. Your team is trained, motivated, and accountable. Customer complaints are rare, and when they happen, you resolve them with grace. You manage labor hours tightly without sacrificing service. |
| **General Manager** | The store hits revenue and margin targets. Your team is engaged, low turnover, high performance. You spot problems before they become crises. You build community partnerships that drive foot traffic. You document learnings so the next GM in the next city can learn from your wins and mistakes. |

### Feedback Culture

We believe in **radical candor**: care personally, challenge directly. We give feedback early, often, and with specificity because we want everyone here to grow, not just survive.

**Examples of good feedback:**
- "Your produce displays are beautiful, but I noticed 3 out-of-date items this morning. Let's talk about how we tighten the rotation process."
- "You handled that difficult customer perfectly—calm, empathetic, and you solved the problem. That's exactly the Uncle May's way."
- "Your labor costs were 2% over budget last week. What happened, and how can we fix it?"

**Examples of bad feedback:**
- "You're doing fine." (Vague, unhelpful)
- "You need to be better." (No specifics, no actionable path forward)
- Saying nothing when performance is slipping, then surprising someone with a negative review later

**Everyone gives and receives feedback**, from the CEO to the newest cashier. Feedback is a gift, not a punishment.

## Living the Culture: Day-to-Day Examples

### **Morning Shift - Stock Associate**
- Arrive 10 minutes early, check yesterday's notes from closing team
- Walk the sales floor: note low-stock items, flag any out-of-date products
- Prioritize restocking high-velocity items before store opens
- Coordinate with Receiving Lead on morning deliveries
- Keep backroom organized and labeled throughout the shift
- **Culture in action:** Ownership (spot problems, fix them), Excellence (shelves full and rotated), Data-driven (track what sells fastest)

### **Afternoon Shift - Cashier**
- Greet every customer warmly: "Welcome to Uncle May's! How's your day going?"
- Scan quickly and accurately, chat naturally (build relationships)
- Promote loyalty program: "Have you signed up for our rewards yet? It's free and you'll earn points on today's purchase."
- Handle a return gracefully: listen, empathize, solve the problem without needing a manager
- Notice long lines, call for backup cashier
- **Culture in action:** Community First (treat customers like neighbors), Excellence (fast, accurate, warm service), Ownership (call for help proactively)

### **Weekly Team Meeting - GM**
- Review last week's numbers: revenue, margin, shrink, labor %, top-selling items
- Celebrate wins: "Produce shrink was 2.1% this week—below target. Great job, [Department Lead]."
- Address gaps: "Labor was 14.5%, slightly over our 12-15% target. Let's talk about why and how we tighten it."
- Solicit feedback: "What's one thing we could improve this week?"
- **Culture in action:** Data-driven (numbers first), Accountability (celebrate wins, address gaps), Feedback culture (open dialogue)

## Onboarding: Your First 90 Days

### **Day 1:**
- Meet the team, store tour, safety training
- Learn the mission: why Uncle May's exists and what we're building
- Understand the culture: read this document, discuss with your manager

### **Week 1:**
- Shadow a high performer in your role
- Learn the systems (POS, inventory, scheduling)
- Ask questions, take notes, observe how great looks

### **Month 1:**
- Master your core responsibilities
- Start contributing ideas: "I noticed X, what if we tried Y?"
- First check-in with your manager: How are you doing? What support do you need?

### **Month 3:**
- You're operating independently, with confidence
- You're training newer team members
- You're contributing to team goals and process improvements
- 90-day performance review: Where do you want to grow next?
