## The Problem

> "Now that I'm on the South Side it's like hunting for food."
>
> *Morgan D., Chicago customer interview, 2026-05-06*

Black households across Chicago face a real, daily friction in feeding their families well:

- **Customers drive long distances** for culturally relevant groceries.
- **Black-owned farm products are difficult to discover**, even for customers who actively want to buy them.
- **Existing grocery delivery services** (chain or national DTC) lack cultural specificity and Black-owned sourcing.
- **Customers want convenience without sacrificing identity.** The trade-off they make today is to sacrifice one or the other.

Uncle May's exists to remove that trade-off.

## Why This Matters

This is more than a grocery business.

- **Reconnecting Black consumers with Black agriculture.** Black farmers represent less than 2% of U.S. agricultural producers (USDA 2022). The supply exists; the channel does not.
- **Closing a food access gap on the South Side.** Customers in our pilot describe their current options in their own words as "hunting for food."
- **Supporting Black farm economics.** Every order routes dollars directly to Black-owned farms instead of through national distributors.
- **Convenience without sacrificing identity.** Customers no longer choose between cultural alignment and grocery convenience.
- **Rebuilding local food trust.** Named farms, named neighborhoods, named delivery days. No anonymity in the supply chain.

## How Uncle May's Works

A simple, transparent operating flow.

**Step 1: Customer orders online by Sunday night.**
Build-your-own catalog. Customer chooses produce and proteins. No subscription, no minimum, no contract.

**Step 2: Orders are aggregated with Black-owned farm partners.**
Anchor supplier today: Run A Way Buckers Club, Pembroke, IL. Additional Black-owned suppliers added as catalog expands.

**Step 3: Produce and proteins are packed in Chicago.**
Hand-packed for each customer. Quality-checked. Branded packaging.

**Step 4: Wednesday delivery across Chicago neighborhoods.**
South Side, North Side, near suburbs. Customers see who is delivering and what they ordered.

**Result:** Fresh, culturally relevant groceries delivered conveniently while supporting Black farmers.

## What We Learned and Changed

*The catalog pivot, told briefly.*

Before April 30 we sold three fixed boxes (Starter, Family, Community). Conversion was poor. Customer interviews and abandoned-cart data showed why: customers could not see the proteins they actually wanted. Linda wanted beef short ribs and saw a generic box. Lia wanted lamb chops and saw a generic box.

We shipped a build-your-own catalog on April 30. Within a week, paid catalog orders began arriving with the proteins customers had been asking for.

Then Miriam told us we should not have retired the community box because she feeds two households. We are bringing it back.

**The lesson:** the product was getting in the way of the demand. We were five weeks into the box business when we found that out, and seven days from finding out to shipping the fix.

**What this means**

**Why this is the use of funds**

The largest line in the use-of-funds table ($14,000 of $25,000) is six months of Chicago paid acquisition with full conversion tracking. We are not buying audience discovery. We have already proved the audience exists. We are buying the time to land the conversion math, with creative iteration, landing-page testing, and packaging improvements running in parallel.

## Appendix B: What This Application Does Not Claim

We are deliberately not claiming things the data does not yet support. Fifth Star and Polsky can rely on this list as a true reflection of what we know and what we do not.

- **No measured retention or LTV yet.** Four interviews and a small number of catalog-era orders is not a cohort. Buying the time to measure LTV honestly is a primary use of this funding.
- **No claim that signals from four interviews will hold across forty.** The right next move is to test convergent customer language as paid creative and measure conversion, not to assume.
- **No long-range revenue forecast in this deck.** This raise is funded against a six-month customer acquisition test, not a multi-year plan.
- **No retail storefront in scope for this funding.** The Hyde Park flagship is a separate, larger raise on a separate timeline. The $25K from Fifth Star buys six months of paid-acquisition truth on the DTC delivery business.

What this application does claim: a functional DTC unit, a clean measurement stack, four Black American customer interviews with convergent verbatim signal, a per-order P&L that reconciles, and a founder who will return to Fifth Star with measured data when the unit-economics targets are met.
