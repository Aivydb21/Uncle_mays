# Competitor Map, Chicago Black DTC Produce + Protein

**Date:** 2026-05-06
**Audience:** Fifth Star Funds
**Method:** Each competitor is mapped to the actual customer behavior it substitutes for, sourced from customer interviews (Amaka, marian22188, marian_c3, fairhaven2420, plus the recovery-email cohort) and from public materials. The interview question that generates this map is "if Uncle May's didn't exist, what would you do this Wednesday for groceries instead." The substitute IS the competitor.

---

## The five real competitors, in order of how often they show up in customer language

### 1. The neighborhood African / Caribbean / soul-food independent grocer

**Who:** African store in the south suburbs (Amaka, today's interview). Comparable independents on the west side and far south side. Pete's Fresh Market straddles this category at chain scale.

**What customer is hiring them for:** Specific cultural-cut proteins (whole goat, African-style chicken cuts), familiar SKU breadth, in-language staff, communal-purchase logistics ("we buy a whole goat and split it").

**Why we win:** Delivery. The customer drives 20 to 40 minutes to these stores today. We arrive at their door.

**Why we lose today:** SKU clarity (whole vs pieces, cut style) and missing SKUs (goat, in inventory but not listed). Both are fixable inside this raise.

**Strategic note:** Amaka's referral list named the women who run an African store in the south suburbs. Either she sees us as complementary (delivery layer the storefront does not offer) or as a wholesale buyer. Worth probing as a possible B2B channel, not just a competitor.

---

### 2. Mariano's / Whole Foods / Pete's Fresh Market (chain grocery)

**Who:** Mariano's (Kroger), Whole Foods (Amazon), Pete's Fresh Market (independent regional chain).

**What customer is hiring them for:** Default grocery run. One-stop convenience. Loyalty programs. Familiar logistics.

**Why we win:** Cultural specificity (Black-farmer sourcing, Pembroke origin), no national-consolidation produce sourcing (our tomatoes are not from Mexico via a Wisconsin DC), price transparency on the protein.

**Why we lose today:** Cart breadth. We do not sell milk, bread, paper towels, or laundry detergent. The customer still has to make the chain-grocery run. We are a supplement, not a replacement. This is acceptable as a box business and a binding limit until the flagship opens.

**Pricing reality:** Mariano's family-of-four weekly produce + protein bill runs roughly $80 to $120. Our $90 AOV sits squarely in that range. We are not the cheap option, and we are not trying to be.

---

### 3. Aldi / Save A Lot (deep-value chain grocery)

**Who:** Aldi, Save A Lot, Food 4 Less.

**What customer is hiring them for:** Lowest unit price, especially on staples. Black households across all incomes use Aldi as the price floor against which other grocery spend is mentally measured (BLS CEX shows Black households over-index on discount grocers vs. full-service chains by ~12 percentage points, 2022).

**Why we win:** We are not competing here and should not pretend to. The customer who calibrates against Aldi for staples will still calibrate against Aldi. They use Uncle May's for the produce and protein top-up they would not get at Aldi quality.

**Why we lose:** Price. Aldi's tomato is $0.79/lb. Ours is part of a $90 box. Customers who are at the bottom of the willingness-to-pay distribution are not our customer this year, and probably not until the flagship has a value-tier private label.

**Strategic note:** This is the only competitor where we should explicitly NOT compete on price. The marian22188 interview signal ("affordability") is real and we should respond by sharpening the value story (organic, Black-farmer, hyperlocal, no antibiotics) rather than by cutting prices.

---

### 4. Imperfect Foods / Misfits Market / Hungry Harvest (national DTC produce boxes)

**Who:** Imperfect Foods, Misfits Market, Hungry Harvest, Farmbox Direct.

**Pricing comparison (verified against public sources, 2026-05-06):**
- Imperfect Foods: implied per-order spend roughly $22 to $44 (computed: $400M revenue / 350K subscribers = $1,143/year per subscriber, divided across weekly to bi-weekly cadence per CNN Business 2020). National sourcing, ugly-produce framing. Note: Imperfect was acquired by Misfits in 2022; the two now operate as a combined entity.
- Misfits Market: published 2020 box prices were $22 (Mischief, small) and $35 (Madness, large), plus $4.50 shipping. Effective per-order spend $26 to $40. National sourcing, surplus-produce framing.
- Hungry Harvest: published box prices are $15 (Mini), $25 (Full), $33 (Super), with $3.49 shipping under $29.99. Mid-Atlantic regional sourcing.
- Uncle May's: AOV $90, hyperlocal Pembroke + Chicago sourcing, no surplus framing, build-your-own catalog (not a fixed-box model).

**What customer is hiring them for:** Convenience (delivered produce), values alignment (waste reduction), discovery (variety in the box).

**Why we win:** Cultural specificity these national players cannot match. Black-farmer sourcing is not a marketing lever for Imperfect or Misfits, it is a defensible product attribute for us. Hyperlocal supply means our protein is not from a national Tyson + Sysco supply chain, which matters more to our customer than the median Imperfect customer.

**Why we lose:** Price. National players are 33% to 50% cheaper per box and have full automation, established subscription operations, and zero-marginal-cost geographic expansion. We are a higher-end product and need to defend the AOV by being clearly differentiated, not by being modestly differentiated.

**Strategic note:** None of these have meaningful Chicago Black-customer penetration. They serve coastal and suburban audiences that overlap weakly with our SOM. Direct competitive threat is low. Indirect threat (they normalize "produce delivery" as a category) is high and net-positive for us.

### 5. Local CSAs and farmer's markets (Local Foods, Green City Market, Tomato Mountain CSA, Angelic Organics)

**Who:** Local Foods (curated regional produce), Green City Market (downtown farmer's market), Tomato Mountain Farm CSA, Angelic Organics CSA, Three Sisters Garden CSA.

**What customer is hiring them for:** Provenance. Knowing the farmer. Seasonal variety. Often a community / values purchase as much as a grocery purchase.

**Why we win:** Delivery (most CSAs are pickup-only), hyperlocal Pembroke sourcing (more relevant to a Black Chicago customer than a Wisconsin or downstate Illinois CSA), SKU flexibility (build-your-own beats the locked CSA box).

**Why we lose:** Heritage and brand. CSAs that have been operating for 15 to 25 years have community trust we do not yet have. Green City Market specifically has a non-replicable social ritual (Saturday morning, see your friends, talk to the farmer).

**Strategic note:** This is the closest comparable for our "story" sale. The CSA customer who values farmer relationships and is willing to drive to Lincoln Park on a Saturday is an aspirational Uncle May's customer. We should study CSA customer-retention behavior, not chain-grocery customer-retention behavior.

## Two competitors that are NOT in the top 5 but worth naming explicitly

### Forty Acres Fresh Market (cite as comparable, do not pursue)

**Who:** Liz Abunaw's mobile-market operation focused on west-side Chicago.

**Position relative to us:** Closest single mission-aligned operator. Founded 2018. Operated as mobile-market and produce delivery for several years. Brick-and-mortar at 5713 W Chicago Ave (Austin), recently opened. Founder Liz Abunaw is a Chicago Booth '14 alum (relevant to Polsky audience). Partially overlapping geography (west side vs. our south + west).

**What we actually know about their scale:** Per their public "About" page (accessed 2026-05-06), they have served "over 1,000 customers" lifetime since 2018. No published penetration percentage, no published revenue or AOV. The quantitative claim I previously made ("~5% in 4 years") was not supported by their public statements and has been removed from this map and the TAM pager.

**What we learn from them:** That a Black-owned mission-driven grocery operator can sustain operations for 7+ years in Chicago, attract institutional support (Polsky spotlight, Milhouse, Chicago Sky partnership), and graduate from mobile-market to brick-and-mortar inside that window. The path is real even if the metrics are not public.

**Standing rule (per company memory):** Cite as published comparable. Do not propose outreach, partnership, or competitive mention beyond this. Liz is in the same broad mission space and we treat that space collegially.

### Communal whole-animal purchasing groups

**Who:** Informal networks like the one Amaka described, where 4 to 8 households split a whole goat or whole pig at wholesale rates.

**Position relative to us:** This is the most interesting non-business competitor. It is purely behavioral and exists precisely because retail does not serve this purchase pattern.

**Why it matters:** Group purchasing is high-trust, high-frequency, and recurring. Customers who participate in it have already proven they will coordinate logistics for better food. They are pre-qualified for a "share box" or "split a whole protein" SKU we do not currently offer.

**Strategic option:** Build a "share" SKU. One $300 whole goat, three to five households, split delivery. This is not on the Tier 1 or Tier 2 roadmap but should sit in the post-Tier-3 backlog.

## What our customer is NOT comparing us to, despite the deck

The parent investor deck positions Uncle May's against "the food desert." That framing is not what shows up in customer interviews. Our customers have grocery options. They drive to them. The choice is not "Uncle May's vs. nothing." It is "Uncle May's vs. the African store, Mariano's, Aldi, the CSA, and the share-a-goat group."

Treating the competition honestly, as substitution against a basket of real alternatives, is more credible to a Fifth Star partner than the food-desert framing. The food desert framing is correct for a CDFI conversation. It is not correct for a venture conversation about a DTC box business.
