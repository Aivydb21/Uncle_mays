# JTBD Memo, Uncle May's Box Business

**Date:** 2026-05-06
**Audience:** Fifth Star Funds (appendix to Slide 4 of the application deck)
**Method:** Five customer interviews conducted between 2026-05-05 and 2026-05-06, plus three feedback-form responses from prior weeks. Interviews used the standard 8-question script (`customer-interviews/script.md`). Five separate respondents across three distinct customer archetypes.
**Sample size disclosure:** Five interviews is enough to identify convergent signal (3+ same-thing) and to surface verbatim language. It is not enough to publish quantitative repeat or LTV claims. The unit-economics memo handles those separately.

---

## The job, one sentence

**When I want to feed my household real food, I want to buy from Black farms without doing the multi-year discovery project it took to find them, so I can support Black economic circulation without trading away convenience or autonomy.**

The three operative phrases are:
- *Real food.* Customer language. Not "fresh," not "organic." Real.
- *Without the discovery project.* The substitute was their own three-year search. We collapsed it.
- *Without trading away autonomy.* No contracts, no subscriptions, no minimums. Volunteered as a #1 driver by 2 of 5 interviewees.

## Convergent signal (3+ interviews)

### 1. "Black farmers" is the #1 emotional driver

Said in those exact words by Miriam ("Y'all had me at all Black farmers"), Antoinette ("buy from Black people all in one place"), and Morgan W ("happy to support a Black farmer"). Implied with equivalent force by Amaka (the brand promise was what got her to click). Four of five.

This is the message that converts. It outranks price, delivery speed, product variety, and customization in unprompted customer language.

**Implication for marketing:** lead all paid creative with this. Marketing copy that buries the Black-farmer message under feature lists is leaving conversion on the table.

### 2. South Side is the customer's geographic identity

Three of five (Antoinette, Morgan W, Morgan D) named the South Side specifically and unprompted. Morgan D went further and named 47th-and-Cottage as the retail gap, which is the same neighborhood the parent deck identifies for the Hyde Park flagship.

**Implication for marketing:** stop calling ourselves a "Chicago" delivery service. We are South Side first. The marketing copy and the paid-acquisition geo targeting should both reflect this.

**Implication for the deck:** the flagship-location narrative is no longer just a founder thesis. It is a customer-named gap in customer-named language.

### 3. No-subscription is a trust signal, not a feature gap

Volunteered as a primary driver by Morgan W ("It's so hard to make adjustments to services and subscriptions these days. You not having that requirement was a big draw for me") and by Morgan D ("Most people set things and then forget, she didn't want to get bogged down").

This is two unprompted #1 placements out of five interviews. The conventional DTC playbook says push subscriptions hard for retention. Our customer is telling us the opposite. They want autonomy as a precondition for trust, and they will repeat on their own cadence (Antoinette has done so 3 times) once they are inside the relationship.

**Implication for product:** keep the no-subscription default. Surface a subscription option as a "save 10%" upsell after the second order, not before.

**Implication for marketing:** "no subscription, no contract, order when you want" should be visible above the fold on /shop and in every paid creative.

### 4. The substitute is in-person grocery, not other DTC

All five customers compared us against in-person grocery (Petes, Aldi, Marianos), farmer's markets, the south-suburbs African store, or "doing all the networking myself." Not one named Imperfect, Misfits, or Hungry Harvest in their consideration set.

**Implication for the deck:** the competitive narrative on slide 7 should de-emphasize the national DTC players. They are a category-defining benchmark for produce delivery as a behavior, but they are not the substitute our customer is choosing between us and. The real competitors are in-person retail and farmer's markets. The map already reflects this; the deck talk track should too.

### 5. Discovery friction was the prior problem

Three of five (Antoinette explicitly, Amaka behaviorally, Morgan D linguistically) describe their pre-Uncle-May's experience as an active search effort. Antoinette: "For at least the last three years, I've wanted to order food from Black farms, but I didn't know how to do it." Amaka drives 30+ minutes each way to the African store. Morgan D calls it "hunting for food."

**Implication for positioning:** the company is the consolidation layer that turned a multi-year personal research project into a single transaction. That framing aligns with the parent-deck thesis (data + distribution platform) and is more defensible to a venture audience than the food-desert framing.

## Surprising single signals (one-of-five, but worth noting)

**Antoinette bought a crock pot specifically because of the whole chicken in our box.** "I've actually never cooked a whole chicken before. But I just got a crock pot, so I was thinking this would be perfect." This is a customer changing their kitchen behavior because of us. It is also a content opportunity (a "first whole-chicken cookalong" series writes itself, and Antoinette's case validates the audience for it).

**Miriam needs the community box back, and we retired it 3 days before her interview.** The 2026-05-03 catalog right-sizing removed the community SKU based on conversion-confusion data. Miriam's interview reveals that the community box was the SKU that fit her actual use case (feeding two households). Decision: bring it back, market it as the multi-household / "feed your household + share" SKU. This is a 4-day decision-and-reversal cycle. The deck's adaptability slide (5) should narrate this honestly.

**Morgan D names the exact retail gap where Hyde Park flagship is planned.** "47th and Cottage kind of area. ... they half a million dollar homes and all these whack grocery stores, she thinks it would be great to be there." Customer-validated flagship location demand. Slot this into Slide 9 (Tier 3 use of funds) where the Hyde Park pre-construction track is justified.

**Amaka would refer to women who run an African store in the south suburbs.** Either she sees us as complementary (delivery layer the storefront does not offer) or she would refer for a finder's-fee model. This is an unusual referral pattern (most customers refer to friends, not to apparent competitors) and worth probing as a possible B2B or affiliate channel down the road.

## Comparison-set patterns

The substitutes named most often, ranked:

1. **Petes Fresh Market**, named by Antoinette, Morgan D. Mid-tier chain with cultural relevance, real produce quality.
2. **Aldi**, named by Antoinette. Price floor.
3. **Marianos**, named by Morgan D. Default chain.
4. **Forty Acres Fresh Market**, named by Morgan D. Closest mission-aligned operator. Customer says "likes them, but too far" and the Chatham location is "too small."
5. **The African store in the south suburbs**, named by Amaka. Cultural-cut proteins.
6. **Farmer's markets**, named by Morgan W and (implicitly) Morgan D for the seasonal window.
7. **"Doing all the networking myself"**, named by Antoinette. Three-year personal research project.

What is NOT in the comparison set, despite the parent deck assuming it would be:

- Imperfect Foods, Misfits Market, Hungry Harvest. Zero mentions.
- Whole Foods. Zero mentions (despite being a Hyde Park anchor).
- Walmart grocery. One mention, and it was Antoinette dismissing it ("I looked disgusted and said: no").
- Online grocery delivery from chain stores (Instacart, etc.). Zero unprompted mentions.

This means the entire DTC-produce-box competitive narrative is largely irrelevant to our actual customer. We are competing inside the Black-cultural-grocery layer, where the relevant players are Petes, Aldi, Marianos, farmer's markets, Forty Acres, and the south-suburbs African store. The DTC players are a category-defining backdrop, not a head-to-head.

## What this memo does NOT claim

- **It does not claim measured retention or LTV.** Five interviews is enough for thematic synthesis, not enough for cohort statistics. The unit-economics memo holds those separately, and explicitly notes LTV is unmeasured.
- **It does not claim the convergent signals will hold across the next 50 customers.** They might. They might not. The right next move is to test the hypotheses (run "Real food. All Black farmers. No contract." as paid creative against the current variant) and watch what happens. Customer interviews generate hypotheses, not proofs.
- **It does not include a survey-grade methodology.** Five conversations conducted in person or by phone, transcribed by founder, no third-party validation. Sample is biased toward customers who replied to a recovery email or who took a delivery in person. Both biases are toward higher engagement than a random sample would show.

These caveats are the honest read. They are also why the founder commitment in the unit-economics memo is to come back to Fifth Star with measured data, not with more interviews.
