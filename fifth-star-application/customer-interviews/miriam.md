# Interview, Miriam Jackson

**Date:** 2026-05-06
**Channel:** in-person, during her Wednesday delivery
**Length:** ~10 min, conversational
**Recording:** no recording, transcript captured by Anthony post-conversation
**Status before interview:** B-bucket, one-time paid customer (Family box, $70, 4/30, 1538 E 94th St)

## One-line summary

Miriam is a high-frequency online grocery shopper for whom "all Black farmers" was the only message that mattered, who bounced because we removed the community box she actually needed for her two-household feeding pattern, and who is one bag-label away from a much higher repeat rate.

## Q1, context

Daily-pattern online grocery shopper. Buys "more as-needed based on what I'm going to cook." Some weeks bigger trips, but most weeks frequent and incremental. **Spends $200 to $300 per week on groceries.** That puts her at the upper end of the Black-household grocery spend distribution we modeled in the TAM pager and confirms the $5,500/year working figure is conservative for our actual customer.

## Q2, trigger

Scrolling social media. Was simultaneously using Too Good To Go (the surplus-food app, picked up a $7 bag right before seeing us). Saw Uncle May's, "Black farmers" caught her eye, decided to check it out.

## Q3, comparison set

Implied: Too Good To Go, Petes Fresh Market, online grocery generally. Did not name a Black-farmer-specific competitor because there is no obvious one in her consideration set. We are filling a gap she was already shopping inside.

## Q4, hesitation

> "Y'all had me at all Black farmers. Honestly, I was like: okay, let me give it a shot and see what this looks like."

**Zero hesitation reported.** Mission alignment alone overcame whatever friction the checkout would otherwise have introduced. This is the cleanest validation of the brand promise from any interview.

## Q5, outcome

Loved the box on receipt. Carrots, potatoes, sweet potatoes, radishes, whole chicken, mixed greens, kidney beans. The product itself was not the bounce reason.

**The bounce reason is operational, not product:** she opened bags and could not tell what some items were. Asked Anthony "what is this?" pointing at the kale.

> "See? Y'all need labels on the bags. Even if you can't fully label everything, having something tied to the receipt that identifies the items would help a lot. At least then I can match the items to what I ordered."

## Q6, the job

Implied (not directly asked in the way the script frames it): the job is "support Black farmers + get good produce in a way that fits how I already shop online." She is shopping online daily anyway. We are an additive line item, not a replacement for her primary grocery flow.

## Q7, repeat

Will repeat conditional on (a) bag labeling fix and, more importantly, (b) the community box being brought back.

> "Why did y'all take the community box down? I was mad. ... Well I need my community box back. I've got this house and another house to feed."

She is feeding multiple households. The Family box was undersized for her actual use case. The community box (which we retired during the catalog right-sizing on 2026-05-03) was the SKU she actually needed.

## Q8, referral

> "I'm telling anybody. It's the Black farmers for me. They need support. That's enough for me right there."

This is the strongest unprompted referral signal in any interview to date. Not "I'd tell my sister" (specific). Not "I might tell some friends" (hedged). It is "anybody" with the reason already loaded.

## Quotes worth using

- "**Y'all had me at all Black farmers.**" — opens the slide on emotional resonance.
- "**I'm telling anybody. It's the Black farmers for me. They need support. That's enough for me right there.**" — referral signal, deck-grade.
- "**See? Y'all need labels on the bags.**" — captured in the moment, validates a specific UX fix.
- "**I need my community box back. I've got this house and another house to feed.**" — single-sentence justification for un-retiring the community box for multi-household customers.

## Implications for product / marketing

- **Ship bag labels this week.** This is a near-zero-cost change (printed labels or twist-tie tags) that closes the single most-cited operational complaint. Should not require any platform engineering.
- **Reconsider retiring the community box.** The 2026-05-03 catalog right-sizing removed it. Miriam is the second customer to flag it (the recovery email cohort had at least one community-box mention). The decision to retire was based on conversion confusion, not on customer demand. The data now says: bring it back, market it as the multi-household / "feeding two houses" SKU.
- **Lead all paid creative with "all Black farmers."** Miriam said this drove her decision twice in the same conversation. Antoinette said the same thing. Morgan W said "happy to support a Black farmer." Three out of three interviews so far rank this as the #1 emotional driver. Marketing copy should not bury it under feature lists.
- **A "what's in your bag" insert** (printed card with the items + descriptions) tied to her receipt would solve the labeling problem without needing per-bag labels. Faster to ship.

## Implications for the deck

- Slide 4: pair Miriam's "all Black farmers" quote with Antoinette's "buy from Black people all in one place" quote to show convergent emotional signal across distinct customer types.
- Slide 5 (adaptability): the catalog pivot story now needs an addendum. We retired the community box, a customer (Miriam) told us within 4 days that was wrong, and we are bringing it back. That is exactly the "we listen and ship" narrative this slide is for.

## Implications for product priorities (slide 6)

- P0: bag labeling (printed insert tied to receipt is fastest path)
- P0: bring back the community box, framed as "feed your household + share with another household"
- P1: cooking guidance for the whole chicken (this also surfaced in Antoinette's interview — she had never cooked a whole chicken before)
