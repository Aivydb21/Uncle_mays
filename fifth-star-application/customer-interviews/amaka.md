# Interview, Amaka

**Date:** 2026-05-06
**Channel:** in-person / phone (Anthony, on-the-ground)
**Length:** brief (informal, not a full 30-min sit-down)
**Recording:** no recording, notes only
**Status before interview:** abandoned cart, IG-source

## One-line summary

Amaka is an African-immigrant Chicagoan who wanted our chicken but bounced at checkout because she could not tell whether she was buying a whole bird or pieces, and she also wants goat. The cultural-specificity gap is the friction, not price.

## Q1, context

Today she gets meat at an African store in the south suburbs. Spends roughly $30 to $40 per trip. Buys "African chicken" there. Buys whole goat at the same store and splits it across a network of friends, more economical that way.

## Q2, trigger

Saw Uncle May's on Instagram. The hook for her was delivery plus chicken. She likes chicken.

## Q3, comparison set

The substitute is the African grocer in the south suburbs. The deeper substitute is the communal goat-buying group she shares meat with. She is already inside a buying network, which is both a competitor (group purchasing absorbs her demand) and an opportunity (referral surface inside a tight community).

## Q4, hesitation

She did not know what kind of chicken she was buying. Was it a whole chicken or pieces. She was not sure what the SKU actually was.

## Q5, outcome (non-buyer)

Did not finish the order. The reason was product clarity, not price, not delivery, not trust. She liked the natural / no-antibiotics framing.

## Q6, the job

If we did not exist she would keep driving to the south suburbs and buying African chicken there, and would keep splitting goat with her friends. We are competing with a habit and a network, not just a store.

## Q7, repeat

Open to ordering if (a) the chicken description is unambiguous (whole vs pieces, and whether the cut style maps to what African shoppers expect), and (b) goat is on the site. Anthony notes goat is in inventory but not currently listed.

## Q8, referral

Without prompting she named her sister AND the women who run an African store in the south suburbs. The store-owner referral is unusual. Either she sees us as complementary (delivery layer that the storefront does not offer), or she would refer for a finder's fee model. Worth probing on a follow-up.

## Quotes worth using

- "She didn't finish the order because she wasn't sure what she was getting."

## Implications for product / marketing

- **PDP copy fix, urgent.** Chicken listing must say "whole chicken, ~X lbs" or "X pieces, X lb each" in the title, not just the name. This is a 30-minute fix and would have saved Amaka's order.
- **List goat.** It is in inventory, it is being asked for, and it differentiates us from the broader DTC produce category. Adding it does not require a new supplier.
- **African-immigrant Chicagoans are a real adjacent segment.** Distinct from the African-American Hyde Park / Auburn Gresham core but overlapping in food culture (whole birds, goat, communal cuts). Worth a dedicated creative test once the catalog clarifies SKU specs.
- **Group purchasing is a behavior to design for.** Multi-household share orders (split a goat, split a case) is how this customer already buys. A "share box" or "split a whole protein" SKU would map directly to existing behavior, not invent a new one.

## Implications for the deck

- Lead the JTBD section with Amaka. She is one of the cleanest "we lost a clearly motivated customer to a fixable PDP bug" stories. That is a credible founder narrative ("here is what we learned, here is what we shipped in response").
- Goat-and-share-buying is the kind of culturally-specific demand signal that justifies the platform thesis on slide 5. National DTC produce companies will not catch this because they are not in the room.
