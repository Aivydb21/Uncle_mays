# Interview, Antoinette Woods

**Date:** 2026-05-06
**Channel:** in-person, during her Wednesday delivery
**Length:** ~12 min
**Recording:** no recording, transcript captured by Anthony post-conversation
**Status before interview:** A-bucket, **3x repeat customer** (highest-value lifetime customer in the box business: $95 + $75 + $72 across 30 days, all Family box). Auburn Gresham, 7919 S Perry Ave.

## One-line summary

Antoinette had been searching for a way to buy from Black farms for three years before finding us, considered Uncle May's the answer she had been waiting for, is already actively evangelizing the brand to anyone who asks, and represents the durable cultural-affinity customer that the entire company thesis is built around.

## Q1, context

Default grocery pattern: **Petes Fresh Market and Aldi.** Always shops first thing in the morning when stores open ("I don't like crowds"). Spends $100+ per trip. Stable, repeating, in-person grocery routine.

She is a chain-grocery-and-discount shopper who supplements with us, not a DTC produce-box shopper who experimented with us. That distinction matters for the deck: she is the customer the parent thesis names ("Black women, age 25 to 35"), not a coastal early-adopter.

## Q2, trigger

Scrolling online. Saw the ad. The cognitive moment was striking:

> "For at least the last three years, I've wanted to order food from Black farms, but I didn't know how to do it. I was trying to figure out: how do you even find these farms? Then I saw this and thought: finally. An opportunity to buy from Black people all in one place. And I could just order it directly without doing all the networking myself."

This is the **discovery-friction** problem statement, in customer language. She had latent demand for years that no existing player solved. Uncle May's is the consolidation layer that turned a research project into a transaction.

## Q3, comparison set

The implicit comparison is "doing all the networking myself." Her substitute was not a competitor; it was a multi-year personal research project that never converted because the discovery cost was prohibitive. We collapsed that cost to one transaction.

This is exactly the platform thesis from the parent deck (data + distribution platform for Black food consumption), validated by a single customer in their own words, before the platform even fully exists.

## Q4, hesitation

> "It felt pretty self-explanatory. I knew I was going to get produce. I remember seeing chicken thighs listed and thinking: okay, I'm happy. Then finding out there was a whole chicken in there made me even more excited."

Zero hesitation. She converted on offer-clarity (chicken thighs visible in the catalog, then upgraded surprise) plus mission alignment. The whole-chicken upgrade was a positive-surprise moment, the kind of small generosity that drives word-of-mouth.

## Q5, outcome

Excited. Bought a crock pot specifically because of the whole chicken.

> "I've actually never cooked a whole chicken before. But I just got a crock pot, so I was thinking this would be perfect."

She is changing her own kitchen behavior because of our box. That is durable. It is also a content/marketing opportunity (a "first whole-chicken cookalong" series writes itself).

## Q6, the job

Implicit job-to-be-done: **circulate dollars inside the Black community while feeding her household real food.** Not "save money on groceries" (Aldi handles that). Not "discover new produce" (Petes handles that). The job is economic-circulation-with-good-food, and we are the only player solving it for her.

## Q7, repeat

Already a 3x repeat. Will continue:

> "I'll definitely order again once I get through this box."

The repeat behavior is observed, not promised. This is the only customer in the dataset where we have measured second-and-third-order behavior in the catalog era.

## Q8, referral

Already actively evangelizing, in real time, before this interview began:

> "I was literally just telling somebody before you pulled up. He asked if I was waiting on a Walmart order. I looked disgusted and said: no. I explained: they gather food from different Black farms and vendors, and you can buy it all together. I'd definitely tell all my family about this."

The Walmart-comparison is unsolicited and specific. She is positioning Uncle May's as the anti-Walmart for Black households. That is on-brand and it is her language, not ours.

## Identity, community, and place

Volunteered without prompting:

> "My family is all right here nearby. I grew up in this house, and my nieces and nephews live nearby too. That's the dream. I also want to grow food in the backyard someday, but I don't really have people around me who think like that. I planted cherry tomatoes one year though."

She is rooted in the South Side, multi-generational household, food-sovereignty-curious, would grow her own food if the social fabric supported it. This is the customer the parent deck is built for. She validates not just the box business but the long-arc thesis (data + distribution + community).

## Implications for unit economics

Antoinette is the only data point we have on **cohort retention behavior in the catalog era.** Three orders in 30 days at $80 average = $240 of revenue from one customer in one month. Annualized run rate at her observed cadence is ~$2,880, against an estimated $40-55 target CAC. That is a ~50x LTV/CAC for one customer. She is not the average and we cannot project her behavior across the customer base, but she demonstrates the upper-bound case the unit-economics memo's "venture-grade" CAC scenario implicitly assumes.
