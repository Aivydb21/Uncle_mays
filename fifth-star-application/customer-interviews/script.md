# Customer Interview Script — Uncle May's Box Business

**Goal:** 7 interviews in 7 days to raise rubric dimension 1 (problem/customer/competitor) from 4 → 7.
**Length:** 30 min, recorded with permission, transcribed.
**Output:** Per-interview notes in `customer-interviews/<name>.md`, then synthesized into `customer-interviews/jtbd-memo.md` for the application.

**Interviewer rule:** the interviewee should talk 80% of the time. If you find yourself explaining what Uncle May's does, stop and ask another question instead.

---

## Opening (2 min)

> "Thanks for jumping on. I'm not selling anything — I'm trying to understand what's happening for people around buying produce in Chicago, and your perspective matters because [reason specific to them]. I'll record so I can listen properly without scribbling notes — that OK? Cool. About 30 minutes, and I'll cut us off at 28 so you're not late to whatever's next."

---

## The eight questions

### 1. Context — what does grocery actually look like for them right now?

> "Walk me through how you got groceries last week. Where, when, what did you buy, what did it cost roughly?"

**Listen for:** stores named, frequency, who shops, cart composition, friction mentions ("I wish," "I had to"), price awareness.

### 2. Trigger — what was happening when they first noticed Uncle May's?

> "Take me back to the moment you first heard of Uncle May's. What were you doing? What were you scrolling? What made you stop on it?"

**Listen for:** platform (IG / FB / referral / search), creative element that landed (photo, copy, promo, founder), emotional state (shopping, browsing, looking for something specific).

### 3. Comparison set — what else did they consider?

> "Before Uncle May's, what other ways had you tried to solve this — getting fresher produce, or supporting Black food businesses, or whatever the underlying thing is for you? What worked, what didn't?"

**Listen for:** named competitors (Imperfect, Misfits, Hungry Harvest, Whole Foods, Aldi, farmer's markets, CSAs, Forty Acres Fresh Market). What killed each option in their head.

### 4. Hesitation — why didn't they buy (or what almost stopped them)?

> "When you were on the page or the checkout, what was going through your head? Was there a moment where you almost backed out?"

**Listen for:** price, delivery window, cart minimum, payment friction, trust signals missing, "do they actually deliver here," "is the produce really fresh."

### 5. Outcome — what actually happened (whether they bought or didn't)?

For buyers:
> "When the box came, what was the first thing you noticed? What did you make with it? What did your household say?"

For non-buyers:
> "What's the actual reason you didn't finish the order? Be brutally honest — you're not going to hurt my feelings."

**Listen for:** unboxing emotion, food prep behavior, household reactions, day-of-delivery experience, technical issue, life-got-in-the-way, second thoughts.

### 6. The job — what were they really hiring this for?

> "If Uncle May's didn't exist, what would you do this Wednesday for groceries instead? And how would that compare?"

**Listen for:** the substitute they'd default to. The substitute IS the competitor. If "I'd just go to Mariano's," Mariano's is the comp; if "I'd skip and order takeout twice," then takeout is the comp; if "I'd drive to Pembroke and buy direct from RAB," that's a different signal.

### 7. Repeat — what would have to be true for them to come back?

For buyers who haven't repeated:
> "What would have to happen for you to order again next Wednesday?"

For non-buyers:
> "If we fixed [the thing they named in Q4], how likely are you to actually come back and try?"

**Listen for:** specific conditions ("if it's under $X," "if there were eggs in it," "if I could pick the exact items"), commitment language vs. polite hedging.

### 8. Referral — would they tell anyone?

> "Without me prompting you — who in your life would you actually text about this? And what would the text say?"

**Listen for:** specificity. "I'd tell my mom" is weak. "I'd text my sister Crystal because she's been complaining about Whole Foods prices" is gold. The exact wording they'd use IS the marketing copy you should be running.

---

## Closing (3 min)

> "Last thing — is there anything I should have asked but didn't? And if I send you a $20 Uncle May's credit as a thank you, where should it go?"

The credit isn't payment for the interview, it's continued contact. About half will use it. The ones who do generate a second-order data point.

## Synthesis rules (for the JTBD memo at the end of the week)

After all 7, group answers by question and look for:

1. **Convergent signal** — the same thing said 3+ times. That's a finding.
2. **Surprising single signal** — one person says something unexpected that lights up. That's a hypothesis.
3. **Verbatim language** — words they actually use, copy-pasted. That's marketing copy.
4. **Comparison-set patterns** — the substitutes named most often are your real competitors.

The JTBD memo should be ≤ 2 pages, mostly bullet points and direct quotes. Keep it brutally tight — partners reading the deck will skim it.
