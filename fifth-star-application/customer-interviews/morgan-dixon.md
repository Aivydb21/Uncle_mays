## One-line summary

Morgan Dixon left the western suburbs for the South Side and discovered her grocery options dropped off a cliff, considers herself a "hunter for food" in the absence of farmer's market season, names Forty Acres (too far) and the missing 47th-and-Cottage retail gap as the geographic context, and ranks no-subscription as the #1 reason she trusted Uncle May's enough to buy.

## Q1, context

Shops at Petes Fresh Market on 43rd and Kedzie. Spends ~$150 per trip on a mix of produce and meats. Lives on the South Side after moving from the western suburbs.

> "Now that she is on the south side it's like hunting for food, it's even worse when it's not farmers market season. Some of the options are terrible for produce so she drives more than she wants to get to Petes."

This is the food-access pain point in customer language, framed as **hunting** (active, effortful) rather than **shopping** (passive, convenient). The contrast with her prior western-suburb baseline makes the gap visible to her in a way it might not be to a lifelong South Side resident.

## Q2, trigger

Scrolling Instagram, clicked on the post. Her two most-shopped Petes locations are South Loop (favorite) and Bronzeville. Both are Petes, both still require a drive from her preferred shopping pattern.

## Q3, comparison set

Named explicitly: **Forty Acres Fresh Market** ("she likes Forty Acres, but it's too far of a drive for her to go to it"), the Chatham location ("too small and is not convenient. It has quick things but she can't get a full cart"), the existing Marianos and Jewel near her ("there is nothing in the fortys and fiftys").

Identifies the geographic gap precisely:

> "47th and Cottage kind of area"

Half-million-dollar homes with what she calls "whack grocery stores" nearby. This is the same gap the parent deck identifies for the Hyde Park flagship, articulated by a customer who lives inside it.

## Q4, hesitation

> "**The most important thing for her is that we don't require a subscription, that is super helpful for her. Most people set things and then forget, she didn't want to get bogged down. She liked everything.**"

No-subscription is her #1 trust signal. This is the second customer (after Morgan Woodthorn) to volunteer this unprompted. The conventional DTC playbook says push subscriptions; both of our deepest interviews say the opposite.

## Q5, outcome

Loved the sweet potatoes and the regular potatoes. Liked the lettuce and the variety. No specific product complaints in this transcript.

## Q6, the job

Going grocery shopping today (the day of the interview) because she is going out of town and they have been eating out, which "doesn't make sense." Will probably go to Petes again. The substitute when we are not the answer is Petes (drive), Forty Acres (too far), or eating out (not sustainable).

The job to be done is "fill the South Side mid-tier neighborhood grocery gap that exists between corner stores and the lone Petes 30 minutes away."

## Q7, repeat

> "She wants to shop again, no need to change things."

Repeat is committed. No conditional fixes required.

## Q8, referral

> "There is a bunch of her friends that she would tell, she has a friend that exclusively shops online for groceries. She already has people who she is keeping posted about our offerings."

She is already in the active referral mode (telling friends) before the interview happened. Names the online-grocery friend as a specific high-conversion target.

## Closing color

Asked if we will continue to deliver (validation that delivery is the #1 product feature for her). Asked about beets and fruit (two product gaps to add to the catalog roadmap).

The 47th-and-Cottage observation is investor-ready language:

> "She is near the Marianos and the Jewel but there is nothing in the fortys and fiftys, and they half a million dollar homes and all these whack grocery stores, she thinks it would be great to be there."

This is **a customer telling us where to put the flagship.** Hyde Park is the planned location and is adjacent to her named gap. Worth tightening the deck's geographic narrative around customer-named neighborhoods rather than abstract demographic cuts.
