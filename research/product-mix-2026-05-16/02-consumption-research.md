# Consumption Research, Product Mix Reset Phase 2

Prepared 2026-05-16 for the Uncle May's Produce product mix rebuild. Source-grounded ranked lists of what US households actually buy, in stores and online, with Black-household-specific signal where the data supports it. Citations in the right column. Sources table at the end.

A note on construction: only two of the four populations have publicly available true rank-ordered top-30 lists (US general produce, and DoorDash US general grocery). The other two lists are synthesized from converging public sources: USDA category data, Numerator soul-food deltas, NHANES race-specific meat consumption, BLS Consumer Expenditure structure, and Instacart NY 2023 as a proxy for online grocery preference. Where a rank is inferred rather than published, the row is flagged "inferred" in the note. Lists shorter than 30 are kept honest rather than padded.

## 6. Paid-source upgrade flags

The free sources support directional decisions. They do not support precise SKU-level ordering or week-by-week purchase frequency. The five upgrades below would each change a specific decision. Costs are public-domain estimates and should be confirmed before commit.

1. **Numerator "Food Culture in the Black Community" full report and Black Consumer Outlook 2026.** The headline figures used in Section 1 (191% mustard greens, 72% black-eyed peas, etc.) come from the public press release. The full report itemizes the top 50+ products by Black-household over-index, basket size, and channel share. **Cost estimate:** Numerator individual reports typically $5,000-$15,000 for non-enterprise buyers, or included in a Numerator Insights subscription ($30,000-$100,000/year). **Decision it would change:** the exact 30 items to lead with, ranked by lift not by inference. Currently 9 of the 30 items in Section 1 are inferred. This upgrade would convert all 30 to data-backed.

2. **Circana OmniMarket Core Outlets category and SKU-level data, Chicago metro, last 52 weeks.** The USDA Weekly Retail Food Sales product (S8) is the public surface of this dataset, aggregated to high-level categories only. The full Circana feed exposes UPC-level dollar and unit sales by retail marketing area. **Cost estimate:** Circana custom pulls $10,000-$50,000 depending on scope; subscription access is six figures annually. **Decision it would change:** SKU-by-SKU ordering for the Hyde Park flagship, including which brands per category (Coca-Cola vs Pepsi share, Tyson vs Perdue share, generic vs branded penetration). Currently no public source gives this.

3. **Nielsen / NIQ Black Consumer Insights subscription, particularly the Black Consumer 360 panel.** Nielsen has a dedicated Black panel that publishes share of voice in fast food (S2 surfaces some of this), retail trip type, and channel choice by income tier. **Cost estimate:** NIQ subscription tiers start around $25,000/year for SMB; targeted reports $5,000-$10,000. **Decision it would change:** the segmentation between "Black Families" and "Black Working Professionals" basket composition. Uncle May's currently treats these as one target; the data likely shows two distinct top-30 lists with different SKU weights (more prepared/ready-to-eat for professionals, more bulk/scratch for families).

4. **Instacart Insights or Ads Manager category data, Chicago DMA.** Instacart sells category dashboards through its Ads Manager. This gives delivery-channel rank by city. The Instacart NY 2023 list (S4) used in Section 2 is a national-press blurb; the Chicago equivalent is hidden behind the ads product. **Cost estimate:** Instacart Ads Manager access is free with a minimum spend commitment (typically $5,000-$10,000 monthly minimum); standalone Insights pricing is not published. **Decision it would change:** Section 2 (Black food-delivery list) precision. Currently Section 2 is the weakest of the four lists. Chicago-specific delivery rank would replace half of the inferred items.

5. **BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey microdata for Chicago MSA, race-disaggregated.** BLS publishes aggregate CEX tables by race; the microdata is free but requires statistical work to disaggregate by city. **Cost estimate:** zero dollars (public dataset); 20-40 hours of analyst time. **Decision it would change:** category-level dollar share (% of grocery budget on meat, on produce, on prepared, etc.) by Black household income tier in Chicago. This is the cheapest upgrade and should be done before any paid pull, because it will calibrate whether the Numerator over-index figures are large enough to outweigh the smaller absolute population.

The strongest single upgrade is #1 (Numerator full report). If only one paid pull is approved, it should be that one. #5 should be done in parallel because it is free.

## Data gaps and honesty notes

Three gaps the CEO should know about before treating these lists as truth:

- Section 2 (Black food-delivery) is the weakest of the four. There is no public ranked list of grocery-delivery items for Black households. It is constructed by overlaying Section 1 (Black in-store) on DoorDash and Instacart NY. The Numerator full report or Instacart Chicago dashboard would correct this in one purchase.
- The NHANES-based meat-by-race data (S6) is from a 2012 publication of 2001-02 survey data. Per-capita race patterns are slow-moving but cuts, brands, and form factors (fresh vs frozen, parts vs whole) have shifted in the last decade. Treat the levels as directional, not exact.
- The IFPA / Circana top 20 (S7) ranks by household penetration percent, not by dollar volume. A 7% household-penetration item like pomegranates is real but small. The general-population list in Section 3 reweights toward the items that show up across multiple sources to avoid the long-tail problem.
